## 11.1 The World's Oldest Dialogue Gets a New Participant

For ten chapters, you have been the student. The Neijing has been the teacher. You learned to eat with the seasons, sleep before Zi Shi, move like water, breathe with intention, and guard your spirit within. The principles are clear. The science backs them up.

Then Monday morning arrives. Your left shoulder has been aching for two weeks. Your sleep is fractured. You had three coffees before noon and skipped lunch because a deadline loomed. You know — intellectually — that the Neijing would have something to say about all of this. But what, exactly? Which pattern fits your situation? Which organ is complaining? Which of the five movements is out of balance?

Twenty-five centuries ago, when the Yellow Emperor faced a health question, he turned to Qi Bo — his court physician, his advisor, his intellectual sparring partner. The Emperor asked. Qi Bo answered. The Emperor pushed back. Qi Bo refined. Through dialogue, understanding emerged.

In 2026, you have a new dialogue partner. It does not wear silk robes or carry acupuncture needles. It runs on silicon and statistics. But it can do something remarkable: it can hold the entire Neijing in its context window while simultaneously cross-referencing modern medical literature, and it can tailor its response to your specific body, your specific complaint, your specific life.

That partner is AI. And this chapter will teach you how to use it — not as a replacement for medical care, not as an oracle, but as a wellness advisor. A modern Qi Bo that you carry in your pocket.

## 11.2 Why AI Is the Perfect Companion for TCM Wellness

TCM's core methodology is individualized pattern differentiation — 辨证论治 (biàn zhèng lùn zhì). Two people walk into a clinic with the same headache. One has a red face, irritability, and a wiry pulse — Liver Yang Rising. The other has a pale complexion, fatigue, and a thready pulse — Blood Deficiency failing to nourish the head. Same symptom. Opposite causes. Opposite treatments.

That kind of multi-variable reasoning is where AI shines. Given enough personal context — your constitution, your symptoms, your lifestyle, your environment — a large language model can generate personalized recommendations that account for multiple interacting factors simultaneously. It cannot palpate your pulse. It cannot examine your tongue coating. But it can synthesize the information you provide and reason through it in ways that a Google search never could.

Here is what AI can do for your wellness practice:

- **Translate principles into protocols.** You know "anger injures the Liver" from Chapter 4. AI can help you design a specific de-stressing routine based on your schedule, your preferences, and your current symptoms.
- **Cross-reference frameworks.** AI can analyze your situation through both TCM and modern medical lenses simultaneously, showing you where the two traditions converge and where they diverge.
- **Track patterns over time.** Feed AI your weekly health data — sleep quality, digestion, mood, energy — and it can spot trends you would miss yourself.
- **Make the Neijing personal.** The Neijing speaks in universals. AI can apply those universals to your particulars.

But boundaries matter. Let's be explicit:

**AI is NOT a doctor.** It cannot diagnose disease. It cannot prescribe medication. It cannot order blood tests or read an MRI. It has no medical license and no malpractice insurance. Every AI system will tell you this, and every AI system is correct.

**AI is a wellness advisor.** It helps you understand principles, design lifestyle protocols, interpret your own patterns, and prepare better questions for your actual doctor. Think of it as the space between "I feel off" and "I should see a physician" — AI lives in that space, helping you make sense of what your body is telling you.

## 11.3 How to Ask AI About Health: Prompt Engineering for Wellness

The quality of AI's output depends almost entirely on the quality of your input. A vague question gets a vague answer. A precise, context-rich question gets a response that can genuinely change your week.

**The bad prompt:**

> "I keep getting headaches. What should I do?"

What AI receives: one symptom, zero context. It will respond with a generic list — drink water, reduce screen time, manage stress, see a doctor. Correct but useless. You already knew all of that.

**The good prompt — the Five-Layer Template:**

Every wellness question you ask AI should contain five layers of information:

**Layer 1 — Symptoms + Timeline.** What exactly do you feel? When did it start? How often? What makes it better or worse?

**Layer 2 — Constitutional Background.** Are you generally a cold or hot constitution? Do you tend toward fatigue (qi deficiency) or restlessness (excess heat)? What are your chronic patterns — poor digestion, cold extremities, night sweats, anxiety?

**Layer 3 — Lifestyle Context.** What does your diet look like this week? How is your sleep? Exercise? Stress level? Screen time? Recent emotional events?

**Layer 4 — What You've Already Tried.** This prevents AI from repeating advice you've already tested.

**Layer 5 — The Ask.** Tell AI exactly what kind of response you want. A TCM pattern analysis? A dietary plan? Acupoint recommendations? A comparison between TCM and Western perspectives?

Here is a complete example using this template:

> **Prompt:**
>
> I've had a dull, heavy headache at the top and front of my head for the past 10 days. It's worse in the morning and after meals, and slightly better after walking outside. I also feel bloated after eating, my appetite is low, and I've been unusually tired despite sleeping 8 hours.
>
> Constitutional background: I'm 38, male, tend toward a cold constitution — cold hands and feet in winter, prefer warm drinks, loose stools when I eat cold or raw food. I've had a "weak digestion" pattern for years.
>
> This week: I've been eating mostly takeout food (greasy, heavy), sitting 10+ hours/day for a project deadline, no exercise for 2 weeks, and I've been anxious about a work presentation.
>
> I've tried ibuprofen (temporary relief only) and drinking more water (no change).
>
> Please analyze this from both a TCM pattern perspective (which organ system, which pattern) and a modern medical perspective. Then suggest a 7-day protocol covering diet, movement, acupoints for self-massage, and any lifestyle adjustments. Keep it practical — I have a demanding job and limited time.

This prompt gives AI everything it needs. The response will likely identify a Spleen Qi Deficiency pattern with Dampness accumulation (脾虚湿困), cross-reference it with tension headache exacerbated by sedentary behavior and poor diet, and generate a protocol that addresses both frameworks simultaneously.

**Three rules for wellness prompts:**

1. **Be specific about your body, not just your symptom.** "Headache" is a symptom. "Dull frontal headache worse after meals in a person with cold constitution and weak digestion" is a pattern.
2. **Always include lifestyle context.** Your symptoms do not exist in a vacuum. They exist in the context of what you ate, how you slept, what you felt, and how you moved this week.
3. **Tell AI what format you want.** "Give me a daily schedule" produces different output than "explain the TCM reasoning." Ask for what you actually need.

## 11.4 Five Real-World Scenarios: AI Wellness in Action

### Scenario 1: Insomnia — When the Spirit Won't Settle

**Copy-paste prompt:**

> I've had trouble falling asleep for the past 3 weeks. I lie in bed for 45–60 minutes before falling asleep. My mind races with work thoughts. Once asleep, I wake 1–2 times and have vivid dreams. I feel tired but wired at bedtime.
>
> Background: 42, female, tend toward yin deficiency — I run warm, have occasional night sweats, dry skin, and feel restless in the evenings. I drink 2 cups of coffee daily (last cup at 2 PM). My job involves intense mental work. I exercise 3x/week (yoga). I eat well but dinner is often late (8 PM).
>
> I've tried melatonin (3mg, minimal effect) and reducing phone use before bed (helped slightly).
>
> Please analyze from both Heart-Kidney Disharmony (心肾不交) and modern sleep science perspectives. Suggest a 2-week sleep improvement protocol: evening routine, dietary adjustments, acupoints, and any supplements worth considering. Separate "definitely helpful" from "worth trying."

**What to expect from AI's response:**
- Identification of Heart-Kidney Disharmony pattern (Heart Fire not descending, Kidney Water not ascending)
- Modern perspective: likely hyperarousal pattern with cortisol rhythm disruption
- Evening protocol: foot soaking, Yongquan + Shenmen acupoint massage, 4-7-8 breathing
- Dietary suggestions: avoiding stimulating foods after 3 PM, adding yin-nourishing foods (pear, lily bulb, white fungus)
- Honest assessment of melatonin limitations and magnesium glycinate as a better option

**Quality checklist — is AI's answer good?**
- [ ] Does it distinguish your specific pattern from generic insomnia?
- [ ] Does it explain *why* the recommendations fit your constitution?
- [ ] Does it address both TCM and modern frameworks?
- [ ] Are the action items specific enough to follow tonight?
- [ ] Does it acknowledge what it cannot determine without clinical examination?

### Scenario 2: Digestive Issues — When the Spleen Is Crying for Help

**Copy-paste prompt:**

> For the past 2 months, I've experienced bloating after every meal, loose stools (2–3 times daily, unformed), low appetite in the morning, and an overall heavy, sluggish feeling. My energy crashes around 2–3 PM daily.
>
> Background: 35, male, cold constitution, tend toward qi deficiency. I've always had a "sensitive stomach." I eat a lot of salads and smoothies because I thought they were healthy. I drink iced coffee daily. My work is sedentary. Stress level: moderate.
>
> I had a basic blood panel last month — everything normal.
>
> Analyze this from the Neijing's Spleen-Stomach theory and the modern gut-brain axis perspective. How might my "healthy" diet actually be hurting me? Give me a 2-week dietary reset plan that specifically strengthens Spleen Qi while remaining nutritionally balanced.

**What to expect:** AI should flag the irony — raw salads and cold smoothies are nutritionally sound by Western standards but disastrous for a Spleen Qi Deficiency constitution in TCM. The Spleen prefers warm, cooked, easily digestible food. Expect a protocol that replaces cold raw foods with warm soups, congee, steamed vegetables, and ginger tea, while explaining the overlap with modern research on digestive enzyme activity (which is temperature-dependent) and the gut microbiome's sensitivity to dietary patterns.

### Scenario 3: Anxiety and Low Mood — The Seven Emotions in Modern Life

**Copy-paste prompt:**

> I've been feeling persistently anxious and low-energy for about 6 weeks. Not severe enough for medication (I asked my doctor), but enough to affect my daily life. I feel a tightness in my chest, sigh frequently, have trouble concentrating, and lose interest in things I normally enjoy. My digestion has also worsened — more bloating, less appetite.
>
> Background: 29, female, tend toward Qi Stagnation. I've always been someone who "holds things in" rather than expressing emotions. I recently went through a difficult breakup. I'm sleeping okay (7 hours) but waking unrefreshed.
>
> Analyze through the Neijing's Seven Emotions framework (particularly Liver Qi Stagnation, 肝气郁结) and modern perspectives on mild anxiety/depression. What lifestyle, movement, dietary, and emotional regulation strategies can support me? I'm not looking for a diagnosis — I have a doctor for that. I want a daily wellness protocol.

**What to expect:** Identification of Liver Qi Stagnation (frequent sighing is a classic sign — the body's attempt to move stuck Qi). Cross-reference with the gut-brain axis (emotional suppression → vagal tone disruption → digestive symptoms). Protocol should emphasize movement (especially walking and stretching to "move Liver Qi"), aromatic foods (citrus peel, mint, jasmine tea), expressive practices (journaling, conversation), and the emotional counter-regulation principle from Chapter 4 — using decisive action to break the stagnation loop.

### Scenario 4: Neck, Shoulder, and Back Pain from Desk Work

**Copy-paste prompt:**

> I have chronic tension in my neck, shoulders, and upper back. It's been building for months. Worse by end of workday, slightly better on weekends. I also get tension headaches 2–3 times per week starting from the base of my skull. I work at a computer 9 hours/day.
>
> Background: 31, male, generally healthy, no chronic conditions. I exercise occasionally (running 2x/week) but do no stretching or mobility work. My posture at my desk is probably terrible. I carry stress in my shoulders.
>
> Analyze from both the Neijing's meridian/fascia perspective (Chapter 9's Tai Yang Bladder channel) and modern myofascial/ergonomic science. Give me: (1) a 10-minute daily mobility routine I can do at my desk, (2) ergonomic adjustments, (3) acupoints for self-massage when pain flares, and (4) any dietary or lifestyle factors that might be contributing.

**What to expect:** Connection between the Bladder meridian (Tai Yang, running from head down the entire posterior chain) and the Superficial Back Line in modern fascial anatomy. Practical desk exercises targeting the upper trapezius, levator scapulae, and suboccipital muscles. Acupoints: Feng Chi (风池, GB20), Jian Jing (肩井, GB21), He Gu (合谷, LI4). Ergonomic checklist matching Chapter 10's sitting principles. Reminder that running without mobility work often tightens the posterior chain further.

### Scenario 5: Seasonal Wellness — Living with Spring

**Copy-paste prompt:**

> It's early spring where I live. I've noticed I feel more irritable than usual, my eyes are slightly dry and itchy, and I've had a few nights of restless sleep with vivid dreams. I also feel an urge to "do everything at once" — a restless, scattered energy.
>
> Background: 45, female, tend toward Liver Qi Stagnation and Blood Deficiency (diagnosed by a TCM practitioner years ago). I follow the Neijing seasonal principles loosely but want to optimize.
>
> Using Su Wen Chapter 2 (四气调神大论) and the Neijing's spring wellness principles, plus any modern chronobiology that's relevant, design a spring wellness protocol covering: sleep timing, diet, emotional regulation, movement, and any specific practices for this season. My goal is to ride spring's rising Yang energy without letting it scatter me.

**What to expect:** AI should reference 「春三月，此谓发陈」 — spring is the season of emergence, when Liver Qi naturally rises. For someone already prone to Liver Qi Stagnation, this rising energy can manifest as irritability, eye symptoms (Liver opens to the eyes), and scattered restlessness. Protocol: sleep pattern shift (夜卧早起 — slightly later to bed, earlier to rise), sour-reducing/sweet-increasing diet to support the Liver-Spleen relationship, gentle stretching and outdoor walking (「广步于庭」from Chapter 10), green vegetables, and emotional practices to channel the rising Qi constructively rather than suppressing it.

**Why this scenario matters:** Seasonal wellness is where the Neijing's principles feel most alien to modern life. We heat and cool our buildings to a uniform 22°C year-round. We eat strawberries in December and pumpkin in July. We wake to the same alarm in every season. The Neijing insists that ignoring seasonal shifts is not just suboptimal — it actively generates disease over years. Asking AI to build a season-specific protocol forces you to reconnect with a rhythm that modern infrastructure has made easy to forget.

## 11.5 Constitutional Self-Assessment: AI as Your Body-Type Analyst

TCM recognizes nine constitutional types (体质, tǐ zhì), classified by Professor Wang Qi of Beijing University of Chinese Medicine. Your constitution is your body's default setting — the terrain on which all symptoms arise. Knowing your type is the single most useful piece of self-knowledge for applying everything in this book.

The nine types:

| Type | Chinese | Core Characteristics |
|------|---------|---------------------|
| Balanced | 平和质 | Energetic, good digestion, stable mood, adaptable |
| Qi-Deficient | 气虚质 | Fatigued easily, weak voice, catches colds often |
| Yang-Deficient | 阳虚质 | Cold hands/feet, prefers warmth, pale complexion |
| Yin-Deficient | 阴虚质 | Runs warm, dry skin/mouth, restless, night sweats |
| Phlegm-Dampness | 痰湿质 | Heavy body, oily skin, sluggish digestion, overweight tendency |
| Damp-Heat | 湿热质 | Oily face, acne-prone, bitter taste, yellow-tinged secretions |
| Qi-Stagnation | 气郁质 | Emotional, sighs often, chest tightness, mood swings |
| Blood-Stasis | 血瘀质 | Dark complexion, easy bruising, fixed sharp pains |
| Special/Allergic | 特禀质 | Allergies, sensitivities, hay fever, skin reactions |

Most people are not purely one type. You might be primarily Qi-Deficient with secondary Phlegm-Dampness, or Yin-Deficient with a tendency toward Qi-Stagnation under stress. AI can help you sort this out.

**Constitutional assessment prompt:**

> I want to determine my TCM constitutional type(s). Please ask me a series of questions covering energy, temperature preference, digestion, sleep, emotional tendencies, skin/complexion, and responses to weather/seasons. After I answer, analyze my responses against the nine constitutional types (九种体质) and identify my primary and secondary types. Then generate a personalized "wellness operating system" — a set of baseline dietary, exercise, sleep, and emotional principles tailored to my constitution that I should follow year-round as my default protocol.

Use this prompt, then answer AI's questions honestly. What you receive back is your personal wellness baseline — a constitution-specific operating manual that contextualizes every principle in this book for your particular body.

Once you have your constitutional profile, add it to the beginning of every future wellness prompt. This single step transforms AI from a generic advisor into a personalized one.

## 11.6 The AI Wellness Journal: Tracking Your Health Evolution

The most powerful use of AI is not a single conversation. It is an ongoing dialogue — a wellness journal where AI serves as your analyst.

**The weekly check-in prompt:**

> Here is my wellness report for this week:
>
> **Sleep:** Average 6.5 hours. Fell asleep easily Mon–Wed, struggled Thu–Sat (work stress). Woke once most nights. Quality: 6/10.
>
> **Digestion:** Mostly good. Bloating after dinner on Thursday (ate heavy hotpot). Stools normal except Friday (loose).
>
> **Energy:** Good Mon–Wed, crashed hard Thursday afternoon, recovered by Saturday.
>
> **Emotions:** Anxious about a presentation (Wednesday). Irritable Thursday evening after the deadline. Calm weekend.
>
> **Movement:** Walked 30 min Mon, Wed, Sat. Yoga Tuesday. No exercise Thu–Fri (too busy).
>
> **Diet:** Mostly home-cooked. Thursday was a disaster — skipped lunch, heavy dinner. Weekend was balanced.
>
> **Notable:** Left shoulder tension flared up again Thursday–Friday.
>
> My constitution type: primarily Qi-Deficient with secondary Qi-Stagnation.
>
> Please analyze patterns, identify what went well and what needs attention, and suggest 2–3 specific adjustments for next week. Compare this week to my previous reports if I've shared any.

Do this every Sunday evening. Over four to six weeks, patterns emerge that are invisible in the moment: your digestion always worsens on high-stress days. Your shoulder tension correlates with skipped exercise, not just desk time. Your sleep deteriorates in a predictable sequence — first you lose exercise, then sleep onset delays, then mood drops two days later.

AI can see these cascading patterns in your data because it does not have the cognitive biases you do. It does not rationalize, deny, or forget. It reads what you wrote and tells you what the data shows.

**A sample AI analysis:**

> Based on your four weeks of reports, a clear pattern emerges: your Thursday–Friday period is consistently your weakest. In 3 of 4 weeks, you skipped exercise on these days, ate poorly, and experienced the highest stress. Your shoulder tension, bloating, and irritability all cluster in this window.
>
> From a TCM perspective, this matches your Qi-Stagnation tendency. Under deadline pressure, Liver Qi stagnates → Qi fails to move → physical tension accumulates → digestion weakens (Liver overacting on Spleen). Your body is telling you that Thursday is not the day to skip your walk — it is the day you need it most.
>
> Suggested adjustments: (1) Make Thursday a non-negotiable 20-minute walk day, even if it means leaving a task unfinished. (2) Prepare Thursday's lunch in advance on Wednesday evening to prevent the skip-lunch-heavy-dinner pattern. (3) Add 2 minutes of shoulder mobility before your Thursday morning starts.

This is not magic. It is systematic self-observation mediated by a tool that never forgets what you told it last week.

**Building your prompt library.** Over time, you will develop a set of go-to prompts for recurring situations: the Sunday check-in, the seasonal transition assessment, the "I feel off but can't pinpoint why" exploratory query, the pre-doctor-visit preparation. Save these. Refine them. A good prompt, like a good question, improves with use. Each iteration teaches you what information AI actually needs and what filler you can trim.

**A note on privacy.** You are sharing personal health information with AI. Understand the privacy policies of the tool you use. Consider using local or privacy-focused AI models for sensitive health data. Never include identifying information (full name, ID numbers, exact address) in wellness prompts — the health context alone is sufficient for AI to help you.

## 11.7 Red Lines: When You MUST See a Doctor

AI enthusiasm has a dark side: the temptation to replace medical care with chatbot conversations. This section is the most important in the chapter. Read it twice.

**AI cannot and should not be used for:**

- Diagnosing any disease or condition
- Recommending prescription medications or herbal formulas with pharmacological effects
- Interpreting lab results, imaging, or clinical tests
- Managing acute or emergency symptoms
- Replacing ongoing treatment plans set by your physician

**Red-flag symptoms — stop asking AI, start calling your doctor:**

| Symptom | Why It's Urgent |
|---------|----------------|
| Chest pain or pressure, especially with shortness of breath | Possible cardiac event |
| Sudden severe headache ("worst headache of my life") | Possible hemorrhagic stroke or aneurysm |
| Unexplained weight loss (>5% of body weight in a month) | Possible malignancy, thyroid disorder, or diabetes |
| Blood in stool, urine, or cough | Multiple serious causes requiring investigation |
| Persistent fever (>38.5°C / 101.3°F for >3 days) | Possible infection requiring identification |
| Sudden vision changes, slurred speech, or limb weakness | Possible stroke — call emergency services immediately |
| New lump or mass anywhere on the body | Requires clinical evaluation |
| Severe abdominal pain, especially if localized | Possible appendicitis, obstruction, or perforation |
| Suicidal thoughts or self-harm urges | Call a crisis line or go to emergency services immediately |
| Any symptom that is severe, sudden, or rapidly worsening | When in doubt, see a doctor |

**How to use AI correctly around medical visits:**

**Before your appointment:** Ask AI to help you organize your symptoms, timeline, and questions. A well-prepared patient gets better care. Prompt: "Help me prepare for a doctor's visit about [symptom]. Organize my information and suggest questions I should ask."

**After your appointment:** If your doctor gave you a diagnosis or treatment plan, ask AI to explain it in plain language, suggest lifestyle modifications that complement the medical treatment, and help you understand what to monitor. Prompt: "My doctor diagnosed me with [condition] and prescribed [treatment]. Explain what this means. What lifestyle and dietary adjustments from a TCM perspective can support my recovery alongside the medical treatment?"

**The principle:** AI works *around* medical care, never *instead of* it. Use AI to be a better-informed patient, not a self-treating one.

One more boundary worth stating plainly: AI does not know what it does not know. Large language models can generate confident, well-structured, scientifically flavored text about topics where they have no reliable training data. This is the hallucination problem, and it applies to health queries as forcefully as anywhere else. When AI recommends a specific herb, acupoint, or supplement, verify it through a second source — a trusted TCM reference, a peer-reviewed study, or your healthcare provider. Trust the reasoning framework AI provides. Verify the specific claims independently.

## 11.8 Reflection Moment

Consider the relationship between the Yellow Emperor and Qi Bo across eleven chapters of this book. The Emperor did not accept every answer passively. He questioned, challenged, asked "why" and "how." Qi Bo explained, reasoned, sometimes admitted the limits of knowledge. The dialogue was collaborative. The final decisions — about policy, about health, about the governance of his own body — belonged to the Emperor.

Your relationship with AI should mirror this dynamic precisely.

You ask. AI answers. You evaluate the answer against your own experience, your own body's feedback, your own common sense. You push back when something does not ring true. You try what seems reasonable and observe the results. You adjust.

Twenty-five hundred years ago, a ruler consulted his physician. Today, you consult an algorithm. The medium changed. The format changed. The spirit of inquiry — curious, critical, self-responsible — did not change at all.

The Neijing's opening chapter declares: 「上古之人，其知道者」— the people of high antiquity, those who understood the Way. They understood because they asked questions and tested answers against lived reality. That is exactly what this chapter asks you to do.

AI gives you access to more knowledge than any individual in human history has ever possessed. But knowledge without personal verification is just data. The gap between data and wisdom is closed by one thing only: your own practice.

## 11.9 Summary & Bridge

This chapter taught you to do something the Yellow Emperor could not: consult an advisor that has read everything, forgets nothing, and is available at three in the morning when your back aches and your mind races.

But AI's power is only as good as the questions you feed it. A vague question produces a pamphlet. A precise, context-rich, constitutionally informed question produces a protocol that feels written for you — because, in a meaningful sense, it was.

You learned five things:

1. **Why AI fits TCM.** Both operate on individualized reasoning. AI's personalization engine and TCM's pattern differentiation are natural partners.
2. **How to ask.** The Five-Layer Template — symptoms, constitution, lifestyle, prior attempts, specific ask — transforms AI from a search engine into an advisor.
3. **What to ask about.** Five real-world scenarios showed you how to construct prompts for insomnia, digestion, mood, pain, and seasonal wellness.
4. **How to build a relationship.** The constitutional assessment and weekly wellness journal turn AI from a one-off tool into an ongoing health companion.
5. **Where to stop.** Red flags, capability boundaries, and the correct relationship between AI and medical care.

The Neijing's opening words — 「昔在黄帝，生而神灵，弱而能言，幼而徇齐，长而敦敏，成而登天」— describe the Yellow Emperor as someone born with spiritual brightness, who from childhood was quick to learn and, upon maturity, ascended to mastery. The path was inquiry. The method was dialogue. The destination was understanding one's own body and one's own nature.

You now have tools the Emperor never imagined. But the path has not changed. Ask. Listen. Test. Adjust. Keep asking.

The wisdom of twenty-five centuries does not expire. It waits for the right question.
